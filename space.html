Chandrayaan-1 was India's first-ever lunar mission and a major milestone in the history of Indian space exploration. Launched on 22 October 2008 by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), the mission marked India's entry into the elite club of nations capable of deep space exploration. The mission was launched from the Satish Dhawan Space Centre in Sriharikota using the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV-C11). Its main objective was to orbit the Moon and gather data about its surface, mineral composition, and topography. Chandrayaan-1 was an orbiter mission, meaning it did not land on the Moon. Instead, it carried a suite of 11 scientific instruments from various international space agencies including NASA, ESA (European Space Agency), and Bulgaria, along with five instruments developed by Indian scientists. The mission's most remarkable achievement came when the Moon Impact Probe (MIP), one of the mission components, was intentionally crashed onto the lunar surface.

ðŸŒ” Chandrayaan-2: Attempt to Soft-Land on the Moon (2019)

Chandrayaan-2 was India's second lunar mission and one of the most ambitious projects undertaken by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). It was launched on 22 July 2019 from the Satish Dhawan Space Centre in Sriharikota using the powerful GSLV Mk III-M1 rocket, India's most advanced launch vehicle at the time. Building upon the success of Chandrayaan-1, this mission aimed not just to orbit the Moon but also to soft-land a lander and deploy a rover, a complex and never-before-attempted feat by India.

The Chandrayaan-2 spacecraft consisted of three components: the Orbiter, the Lander (named Vikram), and the Rover (named Pragyan). The orbiter was equipped with advanced scientific instruments to map the lunar surface and analyze the exosphere. It was designed to function for one year but is still operational and continues to send back valuable scientific data. The lander, Vikram, was designed to make a gentle descent onto the Moon's surface near the south polar region, a place no other mission had targeted before. Once landed, the Pragyan rover was expected to roll out and analyze soil composition, conduct elemental analysis, and study the lunar surface.

Chandrayaan-3 stands as a monumental achievement in India's space history, marking the country's first successful soft landing on the Moon. Launched on 14 July 2023 by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) using the trusted LVM3-M4 rocket, Chandrayaan-3 became a globally celebrated mission when it successfully landed near the Moon's south pole on 23 August 2023. This mission was a direct follow-up to Chandrayaan-2, designed to fix the issues from the previous attempt and prove India's ability to land safely on another celestial body.

Unlike Chandrayaan-2, Chandrayaan-3 was a simplified mission with just two components: a lander named Vikram and a rover named Pragyan. It did not include an orbiter, as the still-functioning orbiter from Chandrayaan-2 was used for communication and observation. The lander was equipped with improved guidance, navigation, and control systems to ensure a safe landing, even in challenging terrain. The mission focused on demonstrating critical technologies for safe landing, such as altitude sensors, hazard detection systems, and throttleable engines.
